# Insight: Nudge Definition And Boundaries

Status: Stable

## Focus

Q-001: What is a nudge in economics and behavioral policy?

## Synthesis

A nudge is a change to choice architecture that predictably influences behavior while leaving options available and without substantially changing economic incentives. The practical idea is to change the setting in which people choose, rather than changing the option set itself or coercing people into a decision.

The boundary matters. A default enrollment rule can be a nudge because people can opt out. A tax, fine, subsidy, or ban is not a nudge because it changes incentives or removes options. Pure information provision is also not the cleanest case of nudging, because it works by informing or persuading rather than by redesigning the choice environment.

Nudging sits between persuasion and regulation. It uses behavioral regularities such as inertia, salience, limited attention, social comparison, and status quo bias to steer choices.

## First Principles Breakdown

Observation: People choose within environments that frame, order, default, simplify, or complicate options.

Mechanism: A nudge changes those environmental features to make one choice more likely.

Assumption: The person retains formal freedom to choose otherwise.

Implication: Ethical and empirical evaluation must ask two separate questions: whether the intervention counts as a nudge, and whether it works or is justified.

## Counterevidence and Limits

The definition can blur at the edges. Some information interventions may be included in broad choice-architecture taxonomies, while narrower ethical accounts exclude mere information from nudging. This synthesis uses the narrower boundary because it preserves the distinction between nudging, persuasion, incentives, and mandates.

Bias checks:

- Substitution: Do not define nudging as any behavior-change policy.
- Anchoring: Do not let defaults stand in for all nudges.
- Overconfidence: The boundary is stable for this source set but contested in the broader literature.

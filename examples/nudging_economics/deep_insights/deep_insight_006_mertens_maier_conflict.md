# Insight: The Mertens Versus Maier Evidence Conflict

Status: Under Review

## Focus

A-001: Mertens versus Maier evidence conflict.

## Synthesis

The Mertens and Maier papers create the central empirical tension in this nudging source set.

Mertens et al. ask whether choice architecture interventions change behavior across domains. Their meta-analysis reports a positive overall effect and finds that effectiveness varies by technique and domain. They also acknowledge publication bias and substantial heterogeneity.

Maier et al. accept the importance of that evidence base but argue that the publication-bias finding should dominate interpretation. Their reanalysis claims that after stronger bias correction, there is no clear evidence for overall nudge effectiveness.

The conflict is not best read as "nudges work" versus "nudges never work." It is better read as:

The broad average effect of nudging is uncertain after publication-bias correction, while some specific nudge types or domains may still be effective.

For policy, this shifts the standard of proof. Instead of citing nudging as a generally validated approach, a policymaker should ask whether the specific intervention type, domain, and implementation context have credible evidence.

## First Principles Breakdown

Observation: Meta-analytic averages can be inflated when published studies overrepresent positive findings.

Mechanism: Publication-bias correction estimates what the evidence might look like if missing null or negative studies were accounted for.

Assumption: Strong policy claims need evidence that survives plausible bias correction.

Implication: Nudging should be treated as a targeted intervention family, not a universally established behavior-change tool.

## Counterevidence and Limits

This synthesis is under review because it does not include replies to Maier et al., later reanalyses, or domain-specific evidence after the critique. The right resolution may differ by nudge category. Decision-structure nudges may be more promising than information or assistance interventions, but this needs better evidence.

Bias checks:

- Substitution: Do not treat "overall effect uncertain" as "all individual nudges fail."
- Anchoring: Do not anchor on either the Mertens headline effect or the Maier headline rejection.
- Overconfidence: The current source set identifies a live dispute, not the final answer.
- WYSIATI: Later literature may change the evidence picture.

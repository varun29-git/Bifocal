# Insight: Nudge Effectiveness Is Contested After Publication-Bias Adjustment

Status: Under Review

## Focus

Q-003: What evidence shows that nudges change behavior in durable or welfare-improving ways?

## Synthesis

The evidence for nudging is not cleanly pro or anti. The broad meta-analysis by Mertens et al. finds that choice architecture interventions change behavior with a small to medium overall effect, and that decision-structure nudges appear stronger than decision-information or decision-assistance nudges. It also finds meaningful variation by domain and technique.

But Maier et al. reanalyze the same evidence base and argue that publication bias is severe enough that no clear overall evidence for effectiveness remains after adjustment. That does not prove every nudge fails. It does mean the broad claim "nudges work" should be treated as under review.

The most honest conclusion is:

Nudges can change behavior, but the general evidence base is fragile. Strong claims should be made at the level of specific nudge types, domains, and study designs, not at the level of nudging as a universal policy tool.

Durability and welfare improvement are even less settled than immediate behavior change in the currently processed source set. The sources mostly support claims about behavior change and ethical acceptability, not durable welfare gains.

  Adds: Reanalyzes the same evidence base and argues that publication-bias correction removes evidence for the overall effect.

## First Principles Breakdown

Observation: A policy tool can have many positive published studies while still having a distorted evidence base.

Mechanism: Publication bias can inflate apparent effects if positive findings are more likely to be published or visible.

Assumption: Policy confidence should depend on bias-adjusted evidence, not only unadjusted meta-analytic averages.

Implication: A responsible nudge policy should be tested locally, preregistered where possible, and evaluated for both behavior change and welfare effects.

## Counterevidence and Limits

The Mertens meta-analysis is broad and quantitatively serious. The Maier critique is also serious but short and focused on publication-bias adjustment. The current source set does not include later replies, corrections, new meta-analyses, or domain-specific evidence after 2022.

Bias checks:

- Substitution: Do not substitute immediate behavior change for durable welfare improvement.
- Anchoring: Do not anchor on the unadjusted effect size or the bias-corrected null alone.
- Overconfidence: The correct conclusion is uncertainty with targeted promise, not blanket rejection.
- WYSIATI: The source set lacks implementation-level case studies and long-term welfare measures.

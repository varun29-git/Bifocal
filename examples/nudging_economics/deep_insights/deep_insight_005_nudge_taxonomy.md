# Insight: Nudges Versus Adjacent Policy Tools

Status: Stable

## Focus

Q-002: How do nudges differ from incentives, mandates, defaults, and information campaigns?

## Synthesis

Nudges differ from adjacent policy tools by how they change the decision situation.

Mandates and bans change the option set. Incentives change the payoff structure. Information campaigns try to change beliefs or reasons. Nudges change the choice environment while leaving options and major incentives intact.

Defaults are not an alternative to nudges. Defaults are one important kind of nudge when people can opt out. For example, a default enrollment rule nudges by making one option happen unless the person actively chooses otherwise.

The useful taxonomy is:

1. Mandate or ban: removes or requires an option.
2. Incentive: changes costs or benefits through taxes, fines, subsidies, rewards, or payments.
3. Information campaign: provides facts, reasons, warnings, or persuasion.
4. Nudge: changes choice architecture, such as defaults, salience, friction, order, reminders, social reference points, or commitment devices.

Some broad choice-architecture taxonomies include decision information as a nudge category, but the narrower ethical definition treats mere information as adjacent to nudging rather than a central case.

## First Principles Breakdown

Observation: Many policy tools aim to change behavior.

Mechanism: They differ by the lever they move: options, payoffs, beliefs, or choice architecture.

Assumption: The policy category should be defined by mechanism, not only by intended outcome.

Implication: Ethical and empirical evaluation should compare nudges to the right alternative. A weak nudge may be worse than a structural reform, but better than doing nothing in a choice environment that already steers behavior.

## Counterevidence and Limits

The boundary between information campaigns and nudges is contested. Labels, warnings, simplification, and framing can both inform and steer. For this reason, the taxonomy should be treated as a practical guide rather than a strict legal classification.

Bias checks:

- Substitution: Do not classify any light-touch policy as a nudge.
- Anchoring: Defaults are central, but they are not the whole category.
- Overconfidence: Real policies can combine nudges, incentives, and information.

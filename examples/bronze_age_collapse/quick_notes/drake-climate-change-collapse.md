# Observation: Climate Stress Is a Plausible Contributor, Not a Complete Explanation

Source: `sources/drake-2012.pdf`
Reviewed: 2026-05-08
Source Date: 2012

## Source Triage

- Type: Primary research and paleoclimate synthesis.
- Author Context: Clear. Brandon L. Drake, Department of Anthropology, University of New Mexico, published in Journal of Archaeological Science.
- Evidence Mode: Compares archaeological occupation patterns with multiple paleoclimate proxy records.
- Use Level: Useful for the climate-stress hypothesis, with caution because the author notes low-resolution climate records.

## Signal

Drake argues that Mediterranean sea surface cooling before 1190 BCE reduced evaporation and precipitation, creating more arid conditions during the Late Bronze Age to Early Iron Age transition. In this model, palatial centers dependent on high agricultural productivity became more vulnerable to social stress, competition, abandonment, and migration.

The paper does not claim that climate directly destroyed societies by itself. It presents climate as a stressor that made existing palatial systems harder to sustain.

## Open Question

Which regions were most vulnerable because of dependence on centralized agricultural surplus, and which regions were resilient?

Concept Candidates:

- Climate stress
- Palatial economy
- Systems collapse
- Greek Dark Ages

## Caveat

The author explicitly warns that many paleoclimate records are low-resolution. This source supports climate influence, but not exact single-year causation.
